Deep space

Deep space object is a term used by amateur astronomers to refer mainly to faint astronomical objects in space outside the solar system, such as star clusters, nebulae and galaxies. These objects are located hundreds to billions of light years from Earth.

All clusters and nebulae observed by amateur astronomers are located in our Galaxy, and there are only a few galaxies available for observation with the naked eye. In order of increasing distance, these are: the Milky Way, the Large Magellanic Cloud (~ 163,000 light years), the Small Magellanic Cloud (~ 200,000 light years), the Andromeda Nebula (~ 2.5 million light years).

There are many catalogs of such objects. So, there is the Messier catalog, which includes a description of 110 objects, or a more complete New General Catalog of more than 8000 objects, or a specialized General Catalog of Galaxies at the Uppsala Observatory. Selections of objects from these catalogs are used by amateurs to test their observational abilities and the capabilities of their astronomical techniques. For example, every year the Messier Marathon takes place, in which participants must see all 110 objects of this catalog in the sky in one night. The "400 Herschel" competition is more difficult, it is intended for more powerful telescopes.
